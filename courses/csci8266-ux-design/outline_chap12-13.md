# Essential Questions:

## 1. Why does the book say you need content strategy?

The book argues that content strategy, as the first thing that customers interact with as a representation of your brand, is critical to making a good first impression. 

Thus, even though stakeholders or clients often ["worry about content later"](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/a-project-guide/9780138188283/ch12.xhtml#ch12lev1sec2:~:text=worry%20about%20content%20later), the book argues that content strategy should be a priority from the beginning of the design process.

## 2. How can understanding content strategy make you better at UX design? 

The authors cite Megan Casey, owner of the company Do Better Content Consulting, who shows the following content lifecycle, which includes the following stages:
1. Strategize
2. Define
3. Create
4. Measure
5. Evolve

With the above in mind, understanding and practicing content strategy can make you a better UX designer because the above cycle has heavy overlap with the UX design process. 
In addition, focusing on content strategy will make you a valuable UX design team member since it is so often overlooked by clients and stakeholders early on in projects.

## 3. What is a wireframe and when should you make one? 

Wireframes are ["low-fidelity (no- or low-code) examples of screens or products, to identify the elements that will be displayed on a screen"](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/a-project-guide/9780138188283/ch13.xhtml#:~:text=low%2Dfidelity%20(no%2D%20or%20low%2Dcode)%20examples%20of%20screens%20or%20products%2C%20to%20identify%20the%20elements%20that%20will%20be%20displayed%20on%20a%20screen).

You should make a wireframe when you want to validate your design meets user needs and JTBD before you make the time commitment to build a prototype.

If approved, they can provide an excellent blueprint for the developers tasked with building the prototype or final product.

## 4. Who are wireframes for? 

Wireframes help users, other UX designers, developres, and stakeholders visualize the design and functionality of a product before it is built.

See [Table 13.1 in the book](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/a-project-guide/9780138188283/ch13.xhtml#ch13tab01) for a breakdown of how different audiences can use a wireframe.

## 5. What does the process of making a wireframe look like? 

The authors outline the following broad appraoch to creating a wireframe:

1. First, consume all the existing information you have about the project, including user research, competitive analysis, content strategy, personas, user stories, and information gathered from stakeholders.

2. Then, sketch out the wireframe on paper or a whiteboard, designing small portions or sections of the project at first.

3. Get feedback on the wireframe from users, stakeholders, and other designers, and iterate on the design based on that feedback.

4. If desired, create a digital wireframe using tools like Figma or Sketch to increase fidelity.

## 6. When is a paper prototype useful? 

Paper prototypes are cheap and flexible for small scale projects. They are also useful for testing out ideas and concepts early on in the design process.

However, they do not handle large or complex projects well, as small tweaks may require complete re-creations of the prototype.

## 7. When is a digital prototype useful? 

Digital prototypes excel at the following:
- handling large, interconnected digital designs
- allowing for quick iterations and changes without needing to recreate the entire prototype, and 
- allowing for more realistic designs (if you have a design library in your prototype tool, the prototype can look very close to the final product, which can be helpful for user testing and stakeholder buy-in).

# Reflection Questions

## 1. Do you agree with the authors' assertion that content strategy is critical to the success of an application? Why or why not? 

I disagree with this assertion. 

Frankly, if content strategy truly were something that was "critical" to the success of an application, then it would not be pushed off until the end of the design process by clients and stakeholders so often.

I'm not saying it isn't important, but I think the authors over-emphasize its importance in most small to medium sized businesses.
These businesses rarely have content strategists on staff, and they are often able to get by with a good UX designer, product manager, and developers who work together to create good content as they go.

## 2. Give an example of a content-focused application and a non-content-focused application you use; which one is more useful to you personally? 

A content-focused application I use is Reddit, which focuses on user-generated content and community engagement.

A non-content-focused application I use is Intellij IDEA, which is an integrated development environment (IDE) that focuses on providing tools and features for software development.

Intellij IDEA is substantially more useful to me personally, as it is a critical tool for my work as a software developer. 
In my experience, content _consumption_ applications like Reddit, Twitter, Instagram, etc. are rarely "required" for users. 
Non-content-focused applications tend to be more focused on JTBD, and thus more "essential" to users' lives.

This is not to say one is inherently better than the other.

## 3. When should you choose a paper prototype or a digital prototype? 

My interpretation of the author's advice is that you should use a paper prototype when:
a) you are in the early stages of the design process and want to quickly iterate on ideas, or
b) you are working on a small, simple project that doesn't require a lot of interconnected screens or complex interactions.

Otherwise, you should move towards a digital prototype, which can survive more dramatic changes and increase in fidelity as the design process goes on.
